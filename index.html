BLOOD TYPING

ABO Blood Grouping

ABO blood grouping describes red blood cells by the presence or absence of type A and type B antigens. Type A and type B antibodies are present in the plasma when the corresponding antigen is absent from the red blood cell. Therefore, type A blood has the type A antigen with type B antibodies in the plasma. Type B blood has the type B antigen with type A antibodies in the blood. Some blood types have both type A and type B antigens present on the red blood cell with no antibodies present. This blood type is called AB. Type O blood has no antigen present on the RBC with both type A and type B antibodies in the plasma. Please see the figure below.

Rh Blood Grouping

The RH blood group system encompasses over 40 different types of antigens. The most common are D, C, c, E, and e. Presence or absence of the D antigen is the most significant for blood typing purposes. Rh-positive blood has the D antigen present on the blood cell. Rh-negative blood lacks the same antigen. Rh-negative blood does not naturally have D antibodies in the plasma. Rh-negative blood can be sensitised if exposed to Rh-positive blood. After this exposure, D antibodies are produced. Severe transfusion reaction will occur if Rh-positive blood is transfused into a person with sensitized Rh-negative blood.

Blood Typing Techniques

Blood obtain through most means is acceptable for blood typing. The most common specimens are obtained through venipuncture and skin puncture. Proper technique must be observed. Grossly hemolyzed specimens should not be used for blood typing. Forward typing is done to determine the antigen present on the blood cells of a specimen. In forward typing antibodies are introduced into patient sample. Agglutination or the clumping of red blood cells is considered a positive reaction for an antigen. Anti-A sera will agglutinate if the red blood cell has the A antigen. Anti-B sera will agglutinate if the red blood cell has the B antigen. Anti-D sera will agglutinate if the red blood cell has the D antigen. The results must be confirmed with reverse typing. Reverse typing uses red blood cell reagents with known antigens to test the type of antibody present in the sample plasma. Reagent A blood contains A antigen blood cells that agglutinate in the presence of anti-A antibodies. Reagent B blood contains B antigen blood cells that agglutinate in the presence of anti-B antibodies. Please see image below for examples of agglutination.

Forward Typing Procedure

  1. Centrifuge blood specimen.
  2. Aliquot two drops of red blood cells into test tube.
  3. Dilute red blood cell aliquot with physiologic saline until 3-5% suspension. Mix.
  4. Label three test tubes: A, B, D.
  5. Put one drop of anti-A sera into the test tube labled A.
  6. Repeat with anti-B sera and anti-D sera into test tubes labelled B and D respectively.
  7. Add one drop of 3-5% red blood cell solution to test tube A, B, and D.
  8. Centrifuge specimen.
  9. Observe and record agglutination.

Reverse Typing

  1. Label two test tubes: A, B.
  2. Gently invert regent A blood and reagent B blood to suspend red blood cells.
  3. Place one drop of reagent A blood into test tube labeled A.
  4. Repeat one drop of reagent B blood into test tube labeled B.
  5. Put two drops of patient plasma into test tube labeled A and test tube labeled B.
  6. Centrifuge specimen.
  7. Observe and record agglutination.
